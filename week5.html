Typography


"Typographic design is one of those fields in design that is taken for granted. Many designers just choose a cool font and away they go" Boutlon (2009)

Typography is the art and technique of arranging type to make written lanaguage readable. There are hundreds of thousands typefaces out there, for picky people this can be a nightmare.

The term typography is also applied to the style, arrangement, and appearance of the letters, numbers, and symbols created by the process

Font Family

There are two types of typefaces. These are:

  • Serif
  • Sans-serif

Serif

"A typeface will have serifs, or it won't. If it doesn't, it's called a sans–serif. If it does, then the serif will either be a bracketed, a hairline, a flare, or a slab serif." Boulton (2009)

Serif are the small lines tailing from the edges of letters and symbols separated into distinct units for typewriter or typesetter, due to this Serif fonts are easier to read in printed work.

This is because the serif make the individual letters more distinctive and easier for our brains to recognise quicker

However serif is harder to read when online, thats why serif is ussualy used for body text because it is more legible and less likely to cause fatigue

Figure 1: Serif Font

Sans Serif

Sans-serif is a typeface that does not have the small projecting features called "serifs" at the end of strokes, due to this san-serif are much better on the web.

An important exception must be made for the web. Printed works generally have a resolution of at least 1000 dots per inch; whereas computer monitors are typically around 100 dots per inch.

i feel that san-serif is the more attractive typeface, much easier to read and much more easier to use and make work on the web.

Figure 2: Sans-Serif Font

Typesetting

Typesetting is the process, the craft, of setting the type for a document. Typesetting is the process of arranging the text and images in a page, in order to prepare them for printing. This includes selecting the most adequate style, size and composition of each and every text chain, photograph and design element.

There are several adjustments that can be made to affect typography.

  • Tracking
  • Kerning
  • Leading
  • Measure
  • Alignment
  • Ligatures

Tracking

Tracking is often confused for kerning, but the concept is a little different. Tracking involves adjusting the spacing throughout the entire word.

You should be very careful when changing the tracking, as it can quickly lead to difficulty in reading.


Kerning

Kerning also adjusts space, but of the distance between two letters. Set too closely together, words are indecipherable; set too far apart, and they're awkward to read.

If some spacing is wide and some narrow, it can be frustrating for someone to read without fully understanding what’s wrong, One of the most important aspects of kerning is to have proportional spacing between each letter, taking into consideration any serifs .


Leading

Leading is an essential design aspect that determines how text is spaced vertically in lines.

For content that has multiple lines of readable text, you'll want to make sure the distance from the bottom of the words above to the top of the words below has appropriate spacing to make them legible.


Measure

Measure is the length of a line of text. For a single-column design measure should ideally lie between 40 and 80 characters.

If the lines are too short then the text becomes disjointed, if they are too long the content loses rhythm as the reader searches for the start of each line.


Alignment

Alignment is an extremely important concept in typography. For some reason, non-designers tend to instinctively center align everything.

In reality, center alignment is the weakest, hardest to read alignment and should be used very selectively.


Hierarchy

"Typographic hierarchy, put simply, is how different faces, weights and sizes of typefaces structure a document" Boulton (2009)

The page as a whole must be designed in a way that clearly communicates to the user what actions are available and how to easily access the information they seek, How to leave comment or buy an item, etc.

One of the most important aspects of communicating a strong typographic hierarchy. The styling and placement of all elements, both type and images should guide the viewer through the content in order of importance.


“Typography exists to honor content.”- Robert Bringhurst: The Elements of Typographic Style

Weight and Width

The weight of a particular font is the thickness of the character outlines relative to their height. A typeface may come in fonts of many weights and heights. The base weight differs among typefaces; that means one normal font may appear bolder than some other normal font.

Some typefaces include fonts that vary the width of the characters, although this feature is usually rarer than weight, Narrower fonts are usually labeled compressed, condensed or narrow.


"The second digit of condensed fonts is a 7. Wider fonts may be called wide, extended or expanded. Both can be further classified by prepending extra, ultra or the like." - Frutiger's system

Subject, Space and Movement

Visual interest ein photographs can come from movement; how the eye moves around the shot. To get the eye moving, the photographer modifies the space around the subject

Figure 11:Subject, Space and Movement- Boultons Example .


The photography has framed the subject on the right, allowing for whitespace, or Looking Room, in the direction the subject is looking. The framing of the subject (1), with the space to the left (2) – the Looking Room – creates movement, shown by the arrow (3)
